# Maricopa County Heat Hazards Infographic

A public-information infographic for Maricopa County, Arizona, designed to communicate heat-related illness risks to a broad audience — including the populations most likely to be affected.

![Maricopa County Heat Hazards Infographic](maricopa-county-heat-hazards.png)

## The headline number

**Heat-related deaths in Maricopa County rose from 61 in 2014 to 645 in 2023** — more than a tenfold increase in a single decade (Maricopa County Department of Public Health, 2024). The infographic exists because most people in the county do not know this.

## The brief

Maricopa County's heat-related death toll has been rising sharply, and the people most at risk are often the hardest to reach with standard public health communication. The challenge was to design a single-page infographic that communicates severity, identifies at-risk groups, and offers actionable prevention strategies, all without overwhelming readers who might have visual impairment, low literacy, or cognitive challenges.

## Audience

The primary audience is the full demographic range of Maricopa County, with deliberate design choices made to ensure the piece works for vulnerable populations first. Beyond the obvious at-risk groups (older adults, people with chronic disease, people experiencing homelessness, people with substance use disorder), the design specifically considers **people without access to air conditioning, transportation, or cooling centers** — a structural-access framing rather than a strictly medical one. If the design works for someone with a visual impairment scanning a flyer at a community center, it works for everyone else too.

## Design decisions

**Two-column layout for balance.** I aligned all information into two columns to create a stable, scannable composition. This gives the eye a predictable path and prevents the cluttered feel that defeats most public-health graphics.

**Color as data, not decoration.** The red-yellow-orange palette is symbolic of the subject — heat — but it's also functional. High-contrast color pairings keep the piece readable for people with visual impairment, and the warm spectrum reinforces the severity of the topic without becoming alarming.

**Sun icons with radiating heat.** Visual reinforcement of the central concept. The icons aren't decorative; they let the reader confirm the topic before reading a single word, which matters for people who scan before they commit to reading.

**Plain language and short, active sentences.** I limited the text to essentials only — no information overload, no jargon. Direct, brief sentences in the active voice keep cognitive load low.

**A two-bar graph for the central statistic.** The 2014-vs-2023 comparison (61 deaths versus 645) is the most consequential piece of information in the entire infographic, and a two-bar chart makes the scale of the change immediate. I built the graph in Google Sheets, then made it transparent, stripped Google's default styling, and replaced the labels with Adobe Express text so it would integrate cleanly into the composition.

**Typographic urgency for the most serious risk.** Within the "Heat-Related Illnesses" list, Heat Stroke is marked **(LIFE THREATENING)** in all caps inside the existing red bubble. This signals priority within the list without breaking the visual system — readers triaging a single section can immediately identify which condition demands fastest action.

**Headlines and information bubbles for clear separation.** Each topic — at-risk populations, heat-related illnesses, prevention, statistics — has its own visual container. The headlines act as wayfinding for readers who jump straight to their question.

**A reference section.** Sourcing matters for credibility on health information. Citations from the CDC, the Maricopa County Department of Public Health, and the NOAA National Weather Service are present in the bottom corner — available for readers who want them, but visually quiet enough not to distract from the primary message.

**Serious tone.** The visual style is approachable, but the writing register is deliberately serious to match the gravity of the subject. Heat death is a real, rising public health issue in Maricopa County. The piece treats it that way.

## Tools

- **Adobe** — primary composition, layout, typography
- **Google Sheets** — graph generation, then exported transparent and restyled in Adobe Express

## What I'd do differently

If I revisited this piece, I would add a Spanish-language version alongside the English. Maricopa County is roughly 30% Hispanic or Latino, and a heat safety message that only reaches English speakers is missing a significant portion of the audience it was designed for — including some of the vulnerable populations the piece prioritizes. I would also test the infographic with one or two readers from the at-risk groups before finalizing, instead of designing for them in the abstract.
