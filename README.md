# Maricopa County Heat Hazards Infographic

I designed a public-information infographic for Maricopa County, Arizona, on the rising risk of heat-related illness. The piece reaches a broad audience, with extra attention to the populations most likely to be affected.

![Maricopa County Heat Hazards Infographic](maricopa-county-heat-hazards.png)

## The headline number

Heat-related deaths in Maricopa County rose from 61 in 2014 to 645 in 2023. That is more than a tenfold increase in a single decade (Maricopa County Department of Public Health, 2024). The infographic exists because most people in the county do not know this.

## The brief

Heat-related illness and death are rising sharply in Maricopa County, and the people most at risk are often the hardest to reach with standard public health communication. I needed to design a single-page infographic that communicated severity, identified at-risk groups, and offered actionable prevention strategies. The piece could not overwhelm readers who might have visual impairment, low literacy, or cognitive challenges.

## Audience

My primary audience was the full demographic range of Maricopa County. I prioritized vulnerable populations in every design decision. Beyond the at-risk groups (older adults, people with chronic disease, people experiencing homelessness, people with substance use disorder), I considered people without access to air conditioning, transportation, or cooling centers. This is a structural-access framing rather than a strictly medical one. If the design works for someone with a visual impairment scanning a flyer at a community center, it works for everyone else too.

## Design decisions

**Two-column layout.** I aligned all information into two columns to create balance. This gives the eye a predictable path and prevents the cluttered feel that defeats most public-health graphics.

**Color as data, not decoration.** I chose a red-yellow-orange palette to reflect the subject matter. High-contrast color pairings keep the piece readable for people with visual impairment, and the warm spectrum reinforces the severity of the topic.

**Sun icons with radiating heat.** I added visual reinforcement of the central concept. The icons let the reader confirm the topic before reading a single word, which matters for people who scan before they commit to reading.

**Plain language and short, active sentences.** I limited the text to essentials only. Direct, brief sentences in the active voice keep cognitive load low.

**A two-bar graph for the central statistic.** The 2014 versus 2023 comparison (61 deaths versus 645) is the most consequential piece of information in the infographic. A two-bar chart makes the scale of the change immediate. I built the graph in Google Sheets, made it transparent, stripped Google's default styling, and replaced the labels with Adobe Express text so the graph integrated cleanly into the composition.

**Typographic urgency for the most serious risk.** Within the "Heat-Related Illnesses" list, I marked Heat Stroke as "(LIFE THREATENING)" in all caps inside the existing red bubble. This signals priority within the list without breaking the visual system. A reader triaging the section can immediately identify which condition demands the fastest action.

**Headlines and information bubbles for clear separation.** I gave each topic its own visual container. The headlines act as wayfinding for readers who jump straight to their question.

**A reference section.** Citations matter for credibility on health information. I cited the CDC, the Maricopa County Department of Public Health, and the NOAA National Weather Service in the bottom corner. The citations are present but visually quiet, so they do not distract from the primary message.

**Serious tone.** The visual style is approachable, but the writing register is deliberately serious to match the gravity of the subject. Heat death is a real, rising public health issue in Maricopa County. The piece treats it that way.

## Tools

- Adobe Express for primary composition, layout, and typography
- Google Sheets for graph generation, then exported transparent and restyled in Adobe Express

## What I would do differently

If I revisited this piece, I would add a Spanish-language version alongside the English. Maricopa County is roughly 30% Hispanic or Latino, and a heat safety message that only reaches English speakers misses a significant portion of the audience the piece was designed for. I would also test the infographic with one or two readers from the at-risk groups before finalizing. Real user input would surface things I missed.
